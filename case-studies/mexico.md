# Mexico Digital Transformation
**American Express · 2016–2017**

## The Original Mandate

Migrate the Mexico market's payment processing to the new global platform — a like-for-like migration. Take what existed locally and move it as-is.

I didn't do that.

---

## Two Weeks on the Ground

Rather than designing a solution from a conference room in Phoenix, I traveled to Mexico and spent two weeks embedded with the back-office operations team. I sat alongside them. I watched how they worked every day.

What I found made clear that a like-for-like migration would be the wrong outcome — it would simply move broken processes to a newer system.

**The Mexico payment reality:**
- Customers made payments through unconventional channels, including physical retail locations like Walmart
- These channels generated data files that were manually processed by a large operations team every single day
- The team's work was labor-intensive, error-prone, and inherently brittle — a manual process at the center of a financial workflow

A platform migration would preserve all of that, just in new infrastructure.

---

## The Proposal

I came back from Mexico with a fundamentally different scope: not a migration, but a **full digital transformation**.

- Eliminate the manual file processing entirely
- Automate the back-office workflows
- Build modern, digital payment infrastructure for the Mexico market

This was more time, more investment, and more engineering scope than what was originally planned. I made the case to leadership — rooted in what I had seen firsthand with the operations team — and got approval.

---

## The Migration Challenge

The digital transformation required a data migration with two distinct datasets, each requiring a different migration strategy. Designing those strategies — handling the differences in data shape, volume, and validation requirements — was one of the more complex technical problems of the effort.

---

## The Downstream Benefit No One Planned For

When Mexico launched SPEI — the country's real-time payment system with stringent SLA requirements for crediting customer accounts — the infrastructure was already in place.

There was no emergency rebuild. No scramble to retrofit real-time payments into a manual processing pipeline. Because we had already digitized everything, the SPEI rollout was an extension of existing digital infrastructure.

The same principle applied when the team expanded to UK Faster Payments and India (data localization compliance). The foundation was already built.

---

## The Leadership Behaviors Demonstrated

**Customer obsession:** I didn't design the solution from a conference room. I went to where the work actually happened before proposing anything.

**Strategic influence upward:** I identified that the original mandate was the wrong goal, made the business case for a better one, and got leadership to change course.

**Proactive scope expansion:** I turned a migration into a transformation because I saw the opportunity — and the risk of not taking it.

---

## The Principle

The best solutions to customer and operational problems come from understanding the actual workflow, not from assumptions made in a planning meeting. The Mexico trip was two weeks and a flight. The downstream value was years of avoided technical debt and immediate readiness for real-time payments.
